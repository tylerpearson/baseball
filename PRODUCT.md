# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Curious newcomers to baseball — new fans, parents, partners of fans, anyone who watches games but has never understood what's actually happening on the mound. They arrive without jargon and want pitching to finally "click." Their context is casual and self-directed: reading on a phone or laptop, often mid-curiosity ("what's a slider?"), with no obligation to finish. The job to be done is comprehension that sticks — they should leave able to name pitches, read a count, and follow why a pitcher throws what they throw. They should feel welcomed and respected, never talked down to and never buried in stats.

## Product Purpose

Pitching 101 is a single-page interactive guide that teaches the craft of MLB pitching from zero: pitch types and their movement, how an at-bat and count work, pitcher archetypes, strike-zone strategy, side-by-side pitch comparison, and the legends who mastered it. It exists because pitching is the most opaque part of baseball for newcomers, and most explanations are either too shallow (a glossary) or too advanced (sabermetrics). Success is a reader who started confused and finishes understanding — and enjoyed the read enough to explore beyond the section they came for. The design is the product: craft, clarity, and reading experience are the value, not a funnel to something else.

## Brand Personality

Premium sports editorial. Three words: **authoritative, inviting, crafted.** The voice is that of a great explainer — confident and precise like a seasoned analyst, but warm and patient like a friend walking you through your first game. Tone is editorial and considered (think a long-form magazine feature), not breezy-blog and not academic. Visually it should feel like a printed sports annual brought to life: dark, rich, typographic, with data presented as something to admire rather than endure. Emotional goals: clarity, quiet authority, and the small thrill of understanding something that used to look like chaos.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS landing page** — no gradient hero, no repeated feature-card grid, no tiny tracked eyebrow above every section, no "hero metric" template.
- **Dry textbook / Wikipedia** — not a flat wall of reference text; explanation must be designed, paced, and visual.
- **Loud ESPN / sportsbook hype** — no garish screaming graphics, ad-cluttered density, or breaking-news energy.
- **Childish / clip-art** — beginner-friendly is not kiddie; avoid cartoonish illustration, emoji soup, and primary-color clip-art despite the audience being new.

## Design Principles

1. **Earn understanding, don't assume it.** Every concept is introduced before it's used; jargon is defined inline. The reader is never expected to already know the thing being explained.
2. **Show the movement, don't just name it.** Pitching is physical and spatial — favor animated trajectories, strike-zone visuals, and comparison over prose whenever a picture teaches faster than a paragraph.
3. **Data as craft.** Stats (velocity, break, usage) are presented as something to admire — typeset deliberately in mono, never dumped in tables for their own sake.
4. **Editorial pacing over uniform sections.** Vary rhythm, density, and section shape so the page reads like a considered feature, not a stamped template.
5. **Authoritative but never intimidating.** Confidence in the explanation, warmth in the welcome — the tone that makes a newcomer feel smart, not small.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target **WCAG 2.1 AA.** Body text ≥4.5:1 contrast against the dark navy surfaces (large/bold text ≥3:1); this matters on the muted blue-gray text tokens, which should stay toward the ink end of the ramp. Full keyboard navigation for interactive elements (pitch comparison, glossary search, strike-zone toggles, tooltips). Every animation — trajectory diagrams, heat-map reveals, transitions — needs a `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` alternative (crossfade or static end state), since motion is central to the teaching. Color is never the sole carrier of meaning (pitch families also use labels/position), supporting color-vision differences.
